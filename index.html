What is HSN Code in GST? A Complete Explanation

The Meaning of HSN

HSN stands for Harmonised System of Nomenclature. It is an internationally standardised system for classifying goods, developed by the World Customs Organisation (WCO) and currently used by over 200 countries. India adopted the HSN system under the GST regime to bring uniformity in product classification across all states and sectors.

Under GST, every supply of goods must be accompanied by the correct HSN code on the tax invoice. This code tells the government ‚Äî and your buyer ‚Äî exactly what product is being sold, which tax rate applies, and how the transaction should be recorded in your GSTR filings.

Why India Uses HSN Under GST

Before GST, India used multiple classification systems under Central Excise, VAT, and Customs. These varied by state and commodity, creating confusion and compliance burdens. When GST was introduced in July 2017, the government standardised product classification using HSN codes to ensure that the same product carries the same tax rate everywhere in India.

HSN codes also facilitate international trade. When an Indian exporter ships goods abroad, the HSN code on the export invoice aligns with global trade standards, making customs clearance smoother and documentation consistent.

Structure of HSN Codes: 2, 4, 6, and 8 Digits

HSN codes are hierarchical. Each additional pair of digits narrows down the product classification further. Here is how the structure works:

Digit Level Name Description Example
2-digit Chapter Broad product category Chapter 61 ‚Äì Knitted apparel
4-digit Heading Product group within a chapter 6105 ‚Äì Men's shirts, knitted
6-digit Sub-heading Specific product type 610510 ‚Äì Of cotton
8-digit Tariff item Most specific classification (used in Customs) 61051010 ‚Äì Men's cotton shirts for export

For GST purposes in India, the level of detail required in HSN codes depends on your annual turnover, as explained below.

Mandatory HSN Requirements Based on Turnover

The Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs (CBIC) has made HSN code requirements progressive based on the taxpayer's aggregate annual turnover in the preceding financial year:

Annual Turnover (Previous FY) HSN Digits Required on Invoice Applicability
Up to Rs. 5 crore 4 digits B2B and B2C invoices
Above Rs. 5 crore 6 digits All invoices
Exporters (all turnover) 8 digits Export invoices and shipping bills

These requirements also apply to HSN summary in GSTR-1 filings. As of April 2021, CBIC mandated HSN code reporting at the invoice level in GSTR-1, making accurate HSN lookup more important than ever.

Legal Background Under GST Law

The requirement to mention HSN codes on tax invoices is governed by Rule 46 of the CGST Rules, 2017. CBIC Notification No. 78/2020‚ÄìCentral Tax (dated 15 October 2020) and subsequent circulars have progressively tightened HSN requirements. Failure to comply with these provisions can lead to incorrect GSTR-1 filings, ITC mismatches, and scrutiny notices.

What is GST Rate and GST Slabs in India?

Understanding GST Slabs

India operates a multi-tier GST rate structure. Unlike a flat-rate system, GST applies different rates to different goods and services based on their nature, usage, and social significance. The five primary GST slabs are:

GST Slab Category of Goods / Services Examples
0% (Exempt) Essential goods, raw food, life-saving drugs Fresh vegetables, milk, unbranded rice, books
5% Basic necessities, processed food, medicines Packaged food, branded paneer, footwear under Rs. 1,000
12% Standard goods, mid-range products Processed food items, business-class air travel, mobile phones (older)
18% Most manufactured goods, services IT services, furniture, cameras, LED lights, restaurant (AC)
28% Luxury goods, sin goods, demerit goods Luxury cars, tobacco, aerated drinks, casinos

Additionally, certain products attract a cess over and above the 28% GST, particularly automobiles, tobacco, and coal. These cess rates are specified under the GST Compensation Cess Act.

Composition Scheme Relevance

Small businesses with annual turnover below Rs. 1.5 crore (Rs. 75 lakh for some states) can opt for the Composition Scheme. Under this scheme, the tax rate is much lower ‚Äî typically 1% for traders, 2% for manufacturers, and 5% for restaurant services. However, composition dealers cannot collect GST from customers, cannot claim ITC, and must file quarterly returns. HSN codes are still required on the Bill of Supply raised by composition dealers.

Special GST Rates

Some goods and services attract rates outside the standard slabs. Examples include:

  • Precious metals like gold and silver: 3%
  • Cut and polished diamonds: 0.25%
  • Affordable housing services: 1%
  • Non-affordable residential housing: 5%

These special rates are defined through specific CBIC notifications and are updated by the GST Council periodically. Using a static PDF to track these rates is risky, which is why dynamic lookup tools remain the most reliable reference.

GST Rates List PDF: What You Need to Know

The government has released multiple GST rate schedule notifications in PDF format. While these are official documents, they are not always updated in real time after GST Council meetings. Rate revisions happen multiple times a year, and a PDF downloaded six months ago may already be outdated for several products. This is a significant risk for businesses relying solely on printed or static PDF resources for their GST compliance.

Why Finding the Correct HSN Code is Critical

Many businesses treat HSN code selection as a formality. It is not. The consequences of using an incorrect HSN code or wrong GST rate can be severe and far-reaching.

Penalties for Wrong GST Filing

Under Section 125 of the CGST Act, a general penalty of up to Rs. 25,000 can be imposed for any contravention of GST provisions for which no specific penalty is prescribed. If the wrong HSN leads to underreporting of tax, the consequences escalate: you may owe the differential tax, interest at 18% per annum, and a penalty of up to 100% of the tax evaded in cases of fraud or willful misstatement.

Input Tax Credit (ITC) Issues

ITC is one of the most valuable benefits under GST. However, it is conditional on correct documentation. If the HSN code on your purchase invoice does not match what your supplier has filed in their GSTR-1, the mismatch can appear in your GSTR-2A or GSTR-2B, putting your ITC claim at risk. A wrong HSN at your end can similarly create disputes with your buyers who rely on your invoice for their own ITC.

GST Scrutiny and Notice Risks

The GST department uses data analytics and automated systems to flag anomalies. If your HSN codes are inconsistent across filings, if your product description does not match the HSN, or if your tax rate does not align with the official rate for that HSN, your business may receive a scrutiny notice under Section 61 of the CGST Act. Responding to such notices requires professional help and consumes time and resources.

Compliance Audits

Under Section 65 of the CGST Act, the GST department can conduct audits of your accounts and records. During such audits, incorrect HSN classification is a common finding. Correcting historical filings after an audit can involve filing amended returns, paying back taxes, and providing detailed justifications ‚Äî all of which could have been avoided with accurate HSN lookup at the time of invoicing.

Complete GST Tax Breakdown with Example Calculations

GST in India is a dual-structure tax: it is shared between the Central Government and the State Government. The way GST is split depends on whether the transaction is within the same state (intra-state) or between two different states (inter-state).

Key Concepts

  • CGST: Central GST ‚Äî collected by the Central Government on intra-state supplies
  • SGST: State GST ‚Äî collected by the State Government on intra-state supplies
  • IGST: Integrated GST ‚Äî collected by the Central Government on inter-state supplies and imports; subsequently distributed between Centre and states

Example 1: Intra-State B2B Transaction

Scenario: A furniture wholesaler in Mumbai sells office chairs to a retailer in Pune (both in Maharashtra).

CGST @ 9% Rs. 900
SGST @ 9% Rs. 900
Total Invoice Value Rs. 11,800

The retailer in Pune can claim Rs. 1,800 as ITC (Rs. 900 CGST + Rs. 900 SGST) against their output GST liability.

Scenario: A consumer in Bangalore buys a mobile phone from a local electronics shop (both in Karnataka).

The Delhi wholesaler can claim Rs. 6,000 as IGST ITC. No CGST or SGST is charged on inter-state transactions ‚Äî only IGST applies.

GST Calculation for Reverse Charge (RCM)

Under the Reverse Charge Mechanism (RCM), the recipient of goods or services ‚Äî not the supplier ‚Äî is liable to pay GST. This applies to specific categories like unregistered dealers supplying to registered businesses, import of services, and certain notified goods. In RCM transactions, the buyer must self-invoice, calculate GST using the same formula, and deposit the tax directly to the government.

RCM GST Amount = (Transaction Value √ó Applicable GST Rate) / 100

Common Mistakes Traders Make While Searching HSN Codes

Despite the availability of both government resources and modern tools, GST non-compliance related to HSN codes remains widespread. Here are the most common errors and how to avoid them.

1. Copying HSN Codes from a Competitor's Invoice

This is one of the most prevalent mistakes, particularly among small traders. Businesses often copy HSN codes from invoices they receive, assuming the classification is correct. However, your supplier may themselves be using an incorrect or approximate code. Always independently verify the HSN code for each product you sell or purchase.

2. Using Outdated GST Rates

GST rates change. Products that were taxed at 28% have moved to 18%, items at 18% have moved to 12%, and some goods have become exempt. Businesses that do not stay updated may continue charging old rates, leading to either short-charging GST (creating liability for the shortfall) or overcharging customers (requiring refund or credit note issuance).

3. Using 4-Digit Codes Where 6 Digits Are Required

As discussed earlier, businesses with turnover above Rs. 5 crore must use 6-digit HSN codes. Using only 4 digits when 6 are required is a non-compliance that can lead to GSTR-1 filing errors and potential notices. Exporters must use 8-digit codes regardless of turnover.

4. Incorrect Product Classification

Some products can plausibly belong to multiple HSN chapters. For example, a nutritional supplement could be classified under pharmaceutical preparations, food products, or chemical compounds depending on its composition and intended use. Misclassification affects not just the tax rate but also import/export duties for cross-border transactions. When in doubt, seek a formal classification ruling from CBIC or consult a GST expert.

5. Ignoring Conditions Attached to GST Rates

Many HSN codes have conditional GST rates. For example, garments below Rs. 1,000 may attract a different rate than those above that value. Footwear has similar value-based conditions. If you do not read the conditions carefully, you might apply the wrong rate even when the HSN code itself is correct.

6. Not Distinguishing Between Goods and Services

Goods use HSN codes; services use SAC (Service Accounting Codes). Confusing the two is a significant error. For example, software as a product (goods) has an HSN code, while software development services (services) use a SAC code. Using an HSN code for a service or vice versa creates misclassification and incorrect tax treatment.

The Case for Government PDFs

CBIC publishes official GST rate schedules as gazette notifications in PDF format. These are the primary legal source for GST rates and are admissible as legal reference in any dispute or audit. GST practitioners have long used these PDFs as their base reference material.

Limitations of PDF-Based Research

Despite their authoritative status, PDFs are far from ideal for day-to-day use:

  • Version control: Multiple amendment notifications exist for the same commodity. The "current rate" may require reading five separate PDFs chronologically.
  • No plain-language search: PDFs list products using technical nomenclature. If you search for "mobile phone," you will not find it ‚Äî it is listed as "transmission apparatus" or "telephonic apparatus" in the HSN schedule.
  • Update lag: GST Council decisions take time to appear as formal gazette notifications. PDFs in circulation may reflect yesterday's rates.
  • No calculation feature: PDFs show rates only. You must calculate tax amounts manually.
  • File size: Complete HSN rate PDFs are large, unwieldy documents that are difficult to search and cross-reference.

The Practical Recommendation

Use government PDFs for reference and legal validation. Use a dynamic online tool for daily operational lookup, invoice preparation, and quick rate confirmation. The two complement each other: the PDF is your legal backing, and the tool is your working instrument.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. How do I find the HSN code by product name?

The most efficient way is to use an online HSN code search tool. Enter the plain English name of your product, and the tool will return a list of matching HSN codes with descriptions and applicable GST rates. Avoid relying on competitor invoices or unverified online sources, as these may be incorrect or outdated.

2. Is it mandatory for small traders to use HSN codes?

Yes. All registered GST taxpayers must mention HSN codes on their invoices. For businesses with annual turnover up to Rs. 5 crore, 4-digit HSN codes are sufficient. For businesses above Rs. 5 crore, 6-digit codes are mandatory. Businesses with turnover below Rs. 1.5 crore were earlier exempt, but CBIC has progressively tightened these requirements ‚Äî 4-digit HSN is now mandatory across the board for all registered taxpayers.

3. How many digits of HSN code are required in India?

It depends on your turnover. Businesses with turnover below Rs. 5 crore need 4-digit HSN codes on invoices. Those above Rs. 5 crore must use 6-digit codes. Exporters must always use 8-digit codes on shipping bills and export invoices, regardless of their domestic turnover.

4. What is the GST rate for mobile phones in India?

Mobile phones (HSN 8517) attract GST at 18%. This rate applies to all smartphones and feature phones, whether sold by retailers, wholesalers, or through e-commerce platforms. The CGST and SGST are 9% each for intra-state sales; IGST is 18% for inter-state sales.

5. How do I calculate GST manually?

Use this formula:
GST Amount = (Product Price √ó GST Rate) / 100
Final Price = Product Price + GST Amount

For example, if a product costs Rs. 1,000 and the GST rate is 18%:
GST = (1,000 √ó 18) / 100 = Rs. 180
Final Price = Rs. 1,180

For intra-state transactions, CGST and SGST are each half of the total GST rate (so 9% + 9% for an 18% rate). For inter-state, the full rate applies as IGST.

6. What happens if I use the wrong HSN code?

Using the wrong HSN code can lead to incorrect GST rates on your invoice, ITC mismatches for your buyer, GSTR-1 filing discrepancies, and potential scrutiny notices from the GST department. In serious cases involving deliberate misclassification to evade tax, penalties can reach 100% of the tax amount and may involve prosecution under GST law.

7. How can I download the GST rates list PDF?

Official GST rate schedule PDFs are available on the CBIC website (cbic.gov.in) and the GST portal (gst.gov.in). Navigate to the Notifications section and look for Central Tax (Rate) notifications. However, given the limitations of PDFs discussed earlier, supplementing PDF downloads with a dynamic online tool is strongly recommended for day-to-day use.

8. What is the difference between HSN code and SAC code?

HSN (Harmonised System of Nomenclature) codes are used for goods. SAC (Service Accounting Code) codes are used for services. Both are 6-digit codes used in GST invoicing and returns. For example, the HSN code for steel pipes is 7304, while the SAC code for construction services is 9954. Using an HSN code where a SAC is required (or vice versa) constitutes a filing error.

9. Can GST rates change?

Yes. GST rates are set by the GST Council, a constitutional body comprising the Union Finance Minister and state finance ministers. The Council meets periodically (typically three to four times a year) and can revise rates up or down for any product or service. These changes take effect from the date specified in the gazette notification. This is why static PDFs and outdated references are risky ‚Äî always verify rates at the time of invoicing.

10. Where can I verify official GST rates?

Official GST rates can be verified at cbic.gov.in (under Notifications & Circulars) and gst.gov.in. The GST portal also has an HSN/SAC search feature. For faster, more user-friendly access, use a dedicated HSN code and GST rate finder tool that aggregates official data in a searchable format.

11. What are the GST rates for textile and garment products?

For readymade garments and apparel, GST is levied as follows: garments and apparel priced below Rs. 1,000 per piece attract 5% GST; those priced above Rs. 1,000 per piece attract 12% GST. The relevant HSN chapters for apparel are 61 (knitted) and 62 (woven). Raw cotton (HSN 5201) is exempt from GST, while cotton yarn (HSN 5205) attracts 5% GST.

12. Is an HSN code the same as a product code or barcode?

No. An HSN code is a tax classification code used for GST purposes. It is a standardised global nomenclature that categorises products into chapters, headings, and subheadings. A product barcode (like EAN or UPC) is a unique identifier for a specific stock-keeping unit (SKU) used in inventory and retail. They serve completely different purposes and should not be confused when filling in GST invoices or returns.

13. Are HSN codes required for services?

No. Services are classified under SAC (Service Accounting Codes), not HSN codes. SAC codes were developed by the Central Statistics Office of India specifically for the GST framework. When invoicing for services, the SAC code and the corresponding GST rate for that service must be mentioned on the invoice.

14. What is the HSN code for packaged drinking water?

Packaged drinking water falls under HSN 2201 (waters, including natural or artificial mineral waters). GST on packaged drinking water in containers exceeding 20 litres is 12%. For packaged water in smaller containers (commonly sold retail), the rate is 18%. Always verify the current rate using an up-to-date tool, as these rates have been revised in the past.
